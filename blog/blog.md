### Introduction

In 2017 two neuroscientists, Eric Jonas and Konrad Kording, ran a [mischievous experiment](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5230747/). Rather than turning the standard toolkit of their field on a brain, they turned it on a MOS 6502, the microprocessor that once ran the original Nintendo Entertainement System (NES) and the Apple II. The contest was deliberately rigged in the scientist's favor: while brains are notoriously poorly understood and hard to study or measure, the 6502 chip can be observed completely, manipulated at will, and is small enough that its full wiring diagram has been known for decades.

Yet, the familiar neuroscience methods (lesion studies, tuning curves, and connectivity analyses) recovered almost nothing of the processor's real organization. They produced findings that looked respectable: regions that responded during particular games, components that seemed to matter. However, none of it amounted to the high-level structure that engineers had deliberately implemented in the microprocessor. The instruments we trust to make sense of the brain could not make sense of a machine we had *already solved*.

The result is unsettling, and exposes an issue that runs in the entirety of natural sciences. We are good at producing simple, localized explanations, but global ones are currently mostly out of reach. And although the literature offers an enormous catalogue of techniques for checking whether a high-level explanation of a complex system is correct, there is no consensus on which of them can be trusted. This is one of the main goals of our articles: we experimentally evaluate which of these techniques are trustworthy.

---

### The problem: validation is hard

The aim of science is more than mere description or prediction. What we want from it is **explanation**: an account of why a system behaves as it does. For anything complex, that account is almost always pitched at a high level. No one explains a gas by following 10^23 molecules through their collisions. Instead, the explanation is written in terms of high-level variables such as *temperature* and *pressure*. Similarly, no one explains an ecosystem one organism at a time, but we do so in terms of populations rising and falling against one another. Most crucially, in the field of AI explainability, we prefer to look for high-level circuits and features rather than explain a trained neural network neuron by neuron.

In every case, the same wager is being made: that some coarse, simplified model captures what the underlying system is really doing. The problem is: what does "really doing" mean? There is no consensus on how to measure this.

This is the gap our earlier work walked straight into. In [*Everything, Everywhere, All at Once*](https://melouxm.github.io/MI-identifiability-web/) we found that mechanistic explanations of even very small neural networks are badly non-unique, with dozens of thousands of incompatible circuits and algorithms all satisfying the field's usual checks even in extremely small networks. One reason we suspected was that the prevailing metrics are too forgiving, and reward explanations that *look* right without any guarantee that they are. This raises the obvious sequel: if today's criteria are too lenient, what would a demanding one look like, and how could anyone recognize it as the real thing?

---

### How to test a metric?

To find out whether a proposed validity metric works, we need systems whose correct explanation is already settled, which is a chicken-and-egg problem.

Human judgment cannot fill the hole either: people reliably favor explanations that are *plausible* (tidy, persuasive, and pleasant to read) over those that are *faithful* (describing the mechanism at work). As such, a wrong account can feel entirely convincing.

Our proposed solution is to work with systems whose correct explanation is not in dispute. Rather than guess at the right account of a complex system, we start from cases where science has long since settled on one: the ideal gas law for a gas, the Lotka-Volterra equations for predator and prey models, and so on. We treat those consensual high-level models as the standard against which a metric is judged.

We thus assembled a benchmark of ten simulated complex systems. Each is a matched pair of a detailed low-level simulation together with the high-level explanation that the relevant community agrees is the right account of it. The collection was chosen to stretch across the whole range of what complexity can mean. Some of the systems are discrete and others continuous, running from Boolean logic circuits at one extreme to molecular dynamics at the other. Some are static, mapping inputs to outputs in a single shot, while others unfold over time.

- The consensually accepted (correct) high-level model

The bar a good metric must clear is therefore double: it has to assign almost no error to the correct explanations, and it has to flag the corrupted ones. The result is a falsifiable test bench, against which any candidate metric can be run to check whether it passes the test.

### A list of candidate metrics, and why most of them fail

We gathered more than thirty candidate metrics of them from multiple fields of science, and arranged them into four families.

The **observational metrics** are the most intuitive ones. The reasoning is that a faithful explanation should reproduce the system's outputs. This family gathers metrics such as the mean squared error, the coefficient of determination, distributional distances such as the KL and Jensen-Shannon divergences, and trajectory-matching tools for systems that evolve in time. These quantities are cheap and usually easy to compute. However, these metrics cannot validate complex explanations, because they cannot distinguish between different mechanisms that lead to the same observable outcome. For example, a student may pass an exam because they understood the key concepts, but also because they memorized the class notes.

The **functional metrics** press further by asking the explanation to reproduce not just the outputs but the *way* those outputs respond when each input is disturbed. Metrics such as Sobol sensitivity indices, fidelity measures, and tests of relational structure belong here. These are strictly stronger than output-matching. However, two systems can share an identical profile of sensitivities while being built along entirely different mechanism. In our example, giving the student more/less time may produce different exam results, without telling us anything about the internal process.

The **information-theoretic** and **representational metrics** look at the internals of the system, asking whether the variables an explanation posits are in fact encoded in the system's internal states. This includes metrics such as probing classifiers, representational similarity, or the information bottleneck. While such measures capture which information is present, they cannot capture how that information is causally transformed. A system can carry exactly the right variables while putting them to entirely the wrong use.

Finally, the **causal and interventional metrics** are the most demanding of the four. They state that an explanation must give the correct answer not under passive observation but under active *intervention*, when an **internal variable** is modified and forced to a value. In mechanistic interpretability, circuits (sparse subgraphs of a neural network) are often found using such metrics.

The observational, functional, and information-theoretic metrics turn out to be diagnostics rather than criteria of validity. They suffer from false positives, letting wrong explanations through often enough that none can be trusted to stand alone for all systems. Many of them, besides, apply only to a narrow band of systems, the temporal measures needing dynamics, others needing finite discrete labels, and so on down the line. Only the causal-abstraction metrics combine accuracy on both halves of the test with the reach to cover everything.

That is a firm endorsement of the causal abstraction program, the proposal that a high-level model earns the title of valid explanation only when interventions upon it correspond faithfully to interventions upon the system beneath.

### Causal abstraction, briefly

Picture two models stacked one above the other. One of them is the *low-level model*, M, which might be the gas of particles, or the transistor netlist, or the trained transformer. The other is the *high-level model*, E: the gas law, the network of logic gates, the symbolic program. They are joined by an *abstraction map* that describes how a low-level state translates into a high-level one: for instance, how a swarm of particles, condenses into a single quantity called *pressure*.

Validity comes down to a commutation operation. Consider a high-level intervention, something like setting the temperature to 300K (~26.85°C). Two routes lead from there to a prediction. By the first, the intervention is applied at the high level and the result translated downward. By the second, the intervention is translated down to the micro-level, the particle simulation is run, and its outcome translated back up. If the two routes always agree, then the high-level model stands as a faithful causal proxy. If they ever diverge, then the explanation is wrong.

### The missing ingredient: *faithfulness*

However, real explanations are selective by their nature. To say that a particular circuit in a network computes the answer is to claim, beneath the surface, that the rest of the network does not. To say that temperature and pressure explain a gas is to claim that the identities of individual particles are beside the point. Mechanistic interpretability rests squarely on this kind of sparsity, the belief that a small subset of components carries the computation while everything else sits idle.

The trouble is that this inactivity is itself an empirical claim, and a strong one, which most metrics never examine. They accept the modeler's account of which variables matter and check consistency only on these. This can allow explanations to look sound even in the presence of causal leaks.

*Faithfulness* testing solves this issue. The principle is to disturb the variables an explanation has dismissed as *irrelevant*, by injecting noise into them or perturbing them outright, and then to confirm that the high-level prediction is not accepted. If modifying a supposed irrelevant variable modifies the answer, then the explanation is wrong.

If perturbing them in any way makes the output no longer agree with the explanation, then the explanation is false.</figcaption>
</figure>

Our metric, the Causal Abstraction Error, or CAE, folds this test into its definition. CAE measures, on a continuous scale, how badly the commutation operation fails, averaged over a distribution of interventions, estimated by sampling. It comes in a top-down form (↓), which begins from high-level interventions and grounds them downward, and a bottom-up form (↑), which begins from low-level interventions and lifts them up, and it can act on several variables at once, catching the interaction effects that a one-variable-at-a-time check would miss. Above all, its pool of interventions always reaches the supposedly irrelevant variables, so that faithfulness is put to the test as a matter of course rather than taken on trust. To gauge the interest of faithfulness, we also define non-faithful (NF) versions of CAE that leave it out.

### Six ways an explanation can secretly be wrong

Our first experiment revealed which metrics are reliable on real-world systems. However, subtler failures may arise from specific contrived cases. We built six such cases, each a minimal trap for causal reasoning.

1. **A hidden confounder.** A variable not included in the operation scales the output, and stays invisible until something perturbs it.
2. **A XOR backup path.** Two supposedly irrelevant variables feed a dormant computation that fires only when both are disturbed together, which makes it a pure test of faithfulness.
3. **A wrong intermediate.** The explanation reaches the right final answer by way of a mistaken middle step, the error masked by a compensating correction downstream.
4. **A spurious mediator.** The explanation has A causing B causing C, when in truth A causes B and C independently, the identical outputs disguising the wrong wiring.
5. **Unreachable states.** Two explanations differ only over a state that the system never visits under ordinary inputs.
6. **A wrong causal direction.** A causal chain is bent into a fork around a common cause, observationally indistinguishable and causally backwards.

Each of these was designed to slip past output-matching. As such, the observational, functional, and information-theoretic metrics fail across the board, since by construction the broken version is indistinguishable from the sound one until an intervention lands in exactly the right place.

The causal family works better. However, this is where the question of operationalization begins to weigh heavily. IIA catches only the fourth and fifth traps and misses the wrong intermediate and the reversed direction that other causal metrics catch without difficulty. The non-faithful variants of CAE, for their part, detect the first two traps, which are exactly the cases that hinge on testing the variables an explanation claims to ignore. Only the full CAE, with faithfulness testing in place, detects all six traps.

### A welcome surprise: the cost is low

A metric is worth having only if it can be afforded, and interventions do not come free, since each one means running the simulation again. A natural question is: *how many of them are required?*

The answer turns out to be reassuring. On nearly every system in the benchmark, the CAE estimate settles down within roughly 30 sampled interventions. More reassuring still, when the systems are scaled up, by compiling Tracr transformers for longer input sequences, the cost does not balloon with the size of the model.

What governs the budget is not the internal complexity of the system but the rarity of the explanation's error. An account that diverges from the truth on only 5% of inputs will be detected after roughly 20 interventions. As such, rare but systematic mistakes stay within reach, however deep or wide the underlying model happens to grow.

### What this means for interpretability, and beyond

This paper confronts the validation problem head-on and offers a constructive reply: a metric that, at least on systems whose truth we know, draws a dependable line between the good explanations and the bad. Several lessons follow from the exercise.

The first, and the one most often repeated and least often heeded in interpretability, is that agreement on outputs is not understanding. A model may match the outputs, match the sensitivities, even encode the right variables in its internals, and still be the wrong explanation. Only interventional, causal criteria escape the issue of equifinality.

The second is that faithfulness cannot be treated as optional. The single design choice that does most to separate a working causal metric from a broken one was whether it tests the variables an explanation had dismissed. Any claim of the form "this circuit does the work and the rest is irrelevant" should handled as a hypothesis awaiting falsification.

The third is that a metric of validity is also a target for discovery. Once a score truly rewards valid explanations, explanations can be sought by optimizing against it, whether by gradient, by symbolic search, by evolution, or with a human in the loop. CAE is meant to serve as exactly that objective.

One point worth nothing is that the CAE judges whether a given explanation is valid. It does not, by itself, prevent a determined modeler from gerrymandering an abstraction map complex enough to work through. Validity is *necessary* for a good explanation without being *sufficient*. Parsimony, the choice of the right level, and the presence of real insight remain separate virtues that this consistency score does not evaluate. In addition, stochastic systems, where the two levels share a common source of noise, cannot currently entirely be handled by our work. These are important next questions to be treated as future work.

The larger point, even so, is one we find hopeful. Fully observable and perfectly manipulable artificial systems make a strange and marvelous laboratory, a place to build and stress-test the science of explanation in the one setting where, for once, the answers are known in advance. The microprocessor defeated the neuroscience of 2017. Perhaps the right metric, sharpened first on the systems we understand, is what will keep that defeat from repeating itself on the systems we do not.
